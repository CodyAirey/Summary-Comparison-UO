Essentially, he stands until he loses feeling and escapes himself.<PARAGRAPH>Siddhartha likes losing himself, but he always finds himself again. The self-denial of the Samanas isn't enough to enlighten him.<PARAGRAPH>Siddhartha asks Govinda  if they're making any progress.<PARAGRAPH>Govinda says they're making lots of progress.<PARAGRAPH>Siddhartha doesn't think so. If the point of all the Feats of Strength is to lose himself, he argues, he could just as easily lose himself in prostitutes and drinking. He tells Govinda that he will leave the Samanas.<PARAGRAPH>Siddhartha and Govinda have spent three years with the Samanas. They learn of a man named Gotama Buddha who has achieved enlightenment. Everyone is gossiping about him.<PARAGRAPH>Govinda thinks they should hear the Buddha's teachings. Siddhartha and Govinda nag each other for a while and then decide to leave the Samanas.<PARAGRAPH>Siddhartha informs the oldest Samana of their decision. The old man gets angry, but Siddhartha hypnotizes him with a powerful glance. The old Samana politely permits them to leave.<PARAGRAPH>Govinda is impressed. Siddhartha has learned a lot from the Samanas. Govinda argues that Siddhartha could have learned to walk on water if he had continued with the Feats of Strength.", "analysis": null, "url": "https://web.archive.org/web/20220323195157/https://www.shmoop.com/study-guides/literature/siddhartha/summary/chapter-2"}
